This work presents the experimental identification and controller design for a propeller-actuated damped pendulum (PADP). Three data sets were acquired and transfer functions were obtained through parameter approximation and MATLAB's System Identification toolbox. Statistical analysis led to the rejection of the sample affected by disturbances, and the final plant model was computed as the average of the remaining valid models. \section{Introduction}

The propeller-actuated damped pendulum (PADP) is a benchmark system in control engineering that exhibits second-order underdamped dynamics driven by a motor-propeller assembly. Its behavior is governed by the interplay between gravitational restoring force, mechanical damping, and the aerodynamic torque generated by the propeller, making it a representative platform for studying identification and control of oscillatory systems \cite{ogata2010}.

Accurate plant identification is a prerequisite for effective controller synthesis. Parametric methods extract the natural frequency $\omega_n$ and damping ratio $\zeta$ directly from measured transient responses, while data-driven tools such as MATLAB's System Identification toolbox fit transfer function models by minimizing a prediction error criterion \cite{ljung1999}. Since experimental data are inherently subject to noise and occasional disturbances, statistical screening of the identified models is essential before model averaging is performed.

Once the plant model is established, different control strategies offer distinct trade-offs between performance, robustness, and implementation complexity. The classical PID controller remains the industrial standard due to its simplicity and intuitive tuning \cite{astrom2006}. Optimal state-feedback control via the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) provides a systematic way to balance control effort and tracking error, and the addition of an integral state allows rejection of constant disturbances \cite{anderson1990}. The I-PD structure — where the integrator acts on the error while the proportional and derivative terms act only on the plant output — eliminates derivative kick and reduces overshoot compared to the standard PID \cite{visioli2006}. Finally, discrete-time implementation is mandatory when the controller runs on a digital platform; the choice of sampling period and discretization method directly affects closed-loop stability and performance \cite{franklin1998}.

This paper reports the complete identification-to-validation cycle for the PADP system. Section~\ref{sec:id} covers experimental data acquisition, parameter approximation, MATLAB-based identification, statistical model selection, and the derivation of the final plant model. Section~\ref{sec:ctrl} presents the design of four controllers. Sections~\ref{sec:results} and~\ref{sec:disc} report and discuss the simulation and experimental results, respectively, evaluated against the design specifications of settling time $t_s \leq 5$ s and maximum overshoot $M_p \leq 3\,\%$.

\subsection{Integral State Feedback Controller (ISF) via LQR}
\label{sec:isf_lqr}

An Integral State Feedback (ISF) controller was designed using the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) criterion. The method augments the plant with an integral state to ensure zero steady-state error and improve disturbance rejection.

The identified plant in controllable canonical form is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.2481 & -15.4300 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 5.2870 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \mathbf{D} = 0
\end{equation}

The system is fully controllable (rank $=2$). To include integral action, the augmented model is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{x}_I = r - \mathbf{C}\mathbf{x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{A}_a =
  \begin{bmatrix}
  -0.2481 & -15.4300 & 0 \\
  1 & 0 & 0 \\
  0 & -5.2870 & 0
  \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \mathbf{B}_a =
  \begin{bmatrix}
  1 \\ 0 \\ 0
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The augmented system is also controllable (rank $=3$). Based on the specifications ($t_s \leq 5$~s, $M_p \leq 3\,\%$), the desired poles were selected as:
\begin{equation}
  p_{1,2} = -2.8 \pm 3.1266j, \quad p_3 = -14.0
\end{equation}

The LQR weighting matrices were chosen using Bryson's rule:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Q} = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\frac{1}{9^2},\;\frac{1}{7^2},\;\frac{1}{0.45^2}\right), \quad
  R = \frac{1}{0.6^2}
\end{equation}

The resulting gain vector $\hat{\mathbf{K}} = [k_1\;\;k_2\;\;K_i]$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{K}} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7214 & 0.4370 & -1.3333 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

which yields the closed-loop poles:
\begin{equation}
  p_{1,2} = -0.2593 \pm 3.9454j, \quad p_3 = -0.4509
\end{equation}

\subsection{ISF-LQR: Simulation--Experiment Discrepancy}

Figs.~\ref{fig:lqr_sim} and~\ref{fig:lqr_exp} show a clear contrast between simulated and experimental responses. The simulation exhibits a slowly damped transient with $t_s = 9.94$~s, consistent with the real pole $p_3 = -0.4509$ near the imaginary axis, which dominates the settling behavior despite the faster complex poles. In contrast, the experimental response converges monotonically to the 0.6~rad reference in $t_s = 3.98$~s with zero overshoot, satisfying both specifications.

A small negative deflection at $t \approx 1$~s in the experimental response (about $-0.07$~rad) is attributed to actuator dead-zone and propeller inertia, which delay the onset of effective control action. This effect is not captured by the linear model.

The faster experimental convergence is consistent with additional nonlinear damping (e.g., friction and aerodynamic drag), which increases the effective damping and suppresses oscillations, leading to a shorter settling time than predicted.

Finally, the experimental response shows effective disturbance rejection; a perturbation at $t \approx 13$~s produces a deviation of approximately 0.25~rad, after which the output returns to the reference, remaining within the margin of less than $2\%$ steady-state error. This confirms the role of comprehensive action in rejecting disturbances despite the model's inaccuracies.

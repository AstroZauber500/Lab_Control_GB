\section{Introduction}


The control of angular velocity in DC motors is a classic automatic control problem
with broad industrial applications. A standard solution is the proportional-integral
(PI) regulator, whose integral action guarantees zero steady-state error for step
inputs and provides disturbance rejection capability~\cite{ogata}.

In this work, a PI regulator is designed for the \textit{hps5130} DC motor system,
modeled as a first-order plant identified experimentally. The design is carried out
in both continuous and discrete time with a sampling period of
$T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$, targeting a 2\% settling time below \SI{300}{\milli\second}
and overshoot below 3\%. Simulation results in \textit{Simulink} and experimental
validation on the \textit{kitprog059} platform are reported~\cite{guia}.


\section{Theoretical Background}



\subsection{DC Motor as a Control Plant}

The DC motor angular speed is modeled as a first-order transfer function:
\begin{equation}
    G(s) = \frac{K}{s + p},
    \label{eq:modelo_motor}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the static gain and $p$ is the dominant pole, both obtained through
experimental identification~\cite{nise}.

\subsection{PI Regulator}

The continuous-time PI controller is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    PI(s) = K_P + \frac{K_I}{s} = \frac{K_c(s - z_0)}{s},
    \label{eq:pi_continuo}
\end{equation}
where $K_P$ sets the response speed, $K_I$ eliminates steady-state error, and $z_0$
is the controller zero whose position relative to the plant pole shapes the
closed-loop dynamics~\cite{nise}.


\subsection{Root-Locus Design}

The closed-loop poles satisfy $1 + K_c \cdot G_{OL}(s) = 0$, where $G_{OL}(s)$ is
the open-loop transfer function. Design specifications impose the following constraints
on the dominant pole location~\cite{nise}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \frac{4}{t_s}, \qquad
    \zeta = \frac{-\ln(M_p/100)}{\sqrt{\pi^2 + \ln^2(M_p/100)}},
    \label{eq:specs}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the minimum required real part, $t_s$ is the 2\% settling time,
and $\zeta$ is the damping ratio corresponding to the maximum overshoot $M_p$.


\subsection{Controller Discretization}

The continuous PI is discretized via the backward Euler approximation
$s \approx (z-1)/(T_s z)$, yielding:
\begin{equation}
    PI(z) = \frac{b_1 z + b_0}{z + a_0},
    \label{eq:pi_discreto}
\end{equation}
where coefficients $b_1$, $b_0$, and $a_0$ depend on $K_P$, $K_I$, and $T_s$.
The chosen $T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$ provides between 30 and 75 samples within
the settling time, ensuring adequate dynamic representation~\cite{ogata,nise}.


\section{Methodology}


\subsection{Plant Model Selection}

Three CSV datasets were collected from the \textit{hps5130} motor under the same
operating conditions. A statistical analysis (mean, standard deviation, and
coefficient of variation) determined the most representative dataset, which was
then used to identify an empirical first-order model via the
\textit{System Identification} toolbox in MATLAB, following \eqref{eq:modelo_motor}.
The RMSE computed over the remaining datasets served as the generalization criterion
for model selection.

\subsection{PI Controller Design}

Three PI controllers were designed in continuous and discrete time using the
\textit{sisotool} root locus editor in MATLAB. Each design placed the controller zero
$z_0$ at a different location relative to the dominant plant pole, yielding three
distinct dynamic responses. All designs were constrained to $t_s < \SI{300}{\milli\second}$
and $M_p < \SI{3}{\percent}$, as imposed by \eqref{eq:specs}. Discrete equivalents
were obtained via \texttt{c2d} at $T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$ and verified in
\textit{Simulink} under step and disturbance inputs.

\subsection{Experimental Validation}

The best-performing discrete-time controller was implemented on the
\textit{kitprog059} platform. Step reference signals and input disturbances were
applied to evaluate settling time, overshoot, and steady-state error under real
operating conditions.

\subsection{Noise Analysis}

The angular speed signal was analyzed during closed-loop operation under a
step input with disturbance, sampled at \SI{100}{\hertz} over a total duration
of \SI{17.87}{\second} (1787 samples). Figure~\ref{fig:noise} shows the
time-domain signal along with its power spectral density (PSD).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/noise_analysis.png}
    \caption{Noise Analysis.}
    \label{fig:noise}
\end{figure}

The signal mean was \SI{3920}{RPM} against a setpoint of \SI{4000}{RPM},
yielding a static error of only \SI{-80}{RPM} ($-2\%$), which confirms
the integral action effectively eliminates steady-state error in practice.
The standard deviation was $\sigma = \SI{490}{RPM}$ and the peak-to-peak
amplitude reached \SI{4390}{RPM}, representing 12.3\% of the reference
speed. The RMSE with respect to the setpoint was \SI{500}{RPM},
consistent with the $\sigma$ value given the near-zero mean error.

Spectral analysis revealed a dominant frequency component at
\SI{0.168}{\hertz} with an amplitude of \SI{190}{RPM}, suggesting the
disturbance is not purely random but contains a low-frequency oscillatory
component, likely associated with mechanical load periodicity or
commutator switching. The total noise power estimated from the PSD was
\SI{2e4}{RPM\squared}, yielding a signal-to-noise ratio of
\SI{18.04}{\decibel}. This moderate SNR explains the high coefficient
of variation observed for $\tau$ in velocity model identification
(CV = 47.14\%): the transient slope estimation is sensitive to the
low-frequency component at \SI{0.168}{\hertz}, which distorts the
apparent settling behavior and inflates the variance of $\tau$ across
repetitions. The current model, whose transient is faster and less
affected by this component, showed a much lower variability
(CV = 4.61\% for $\tau$). These results suggest that a pre-identification
low-pass filter or signal averaging could reduce $\tau$ uncertainty in
future experiments.

\subsection{Experimental versus Simulation Results}

The experimental results reveal discrepancies with respect to simulation predictions,
as expected from a first-order model that does not capture friction, dead zone, or
amplifier nonlinearities.
 
For the continuous-time controllers, the experimental settling times were
\SI{330}{\milli\second}, \SI{315}{\milli\second}, and \SI{330}{\milli\second} for
PI$_1$, PI$_2$, and PI$_3$ respectively, compared to simulated values of
\SI{310}{\milli\second} and \SI{391}{\milli\second} for PI$_1$ and PI$_2$. The
deviations are moderate and within the same order of magnitude, indicating that the
identified first-order model captures the dominant dynamics adequately. All three
continuous-time controllers remained within the \SI{3}{\percent} overshoot
specification experimentally (0.95\%, 0.83\%, and 1.26\% for PI$_1$, PI$_2$, and
PI$_3$ respectively), confirming the validity of the root-locus design for this
implementation domain. The non-zero steady-state error observed ($e_{ss} \approx
\pm 0.02$--$0.03$) is attributed to sensor quantization and measurement noise at the
operating speed, rather than a structural failure of the integral action.
 
The discrete-time controllers showed a more pronounced divergence from simulation.
PI$_2$ and PI$_3$ met the overshoot specification with 2.0\% and 1.45\% respectively,
and achieved settling times of \SI{180}{\milli\second} and \SI{230}{\milli\second},
both below the simulated values, suggesting that the real plant dynamics are slightly
faster than the identified model at this operating point. In contrast, PI$_1$ exhibited
an overshoot of 23.4\%, far exceeding the \SI{3}{\percent} specification despite
meeting it in simulation (\SI{0.49}{\percent}). This anomalous behavior is consistent
with the zero placement of PI$_1$ being closest to the plant pole, which makes the
closed-loop response more sensitive to model mismatch and to the quantization effects
inherent in discrete implementation at $T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$. The interaction
between the low-frequency noise component identified at \SI{0.168}{\hertz} and the
aggressive gain of PI$_1$ likely amplified the transient overshoot under real
conditions. PI$_2$ therefore emerges as the best overall discrete-time design,
satisfying all specifications in both simulation and experiment with the best
generalization to the real plant.

Additionally, a discrepancy was observed when comparing the performance metrics
reported in Tables~\ref{tab:sim_performance} and~\ref{tab:exp_performance} with
those obtained from the graphical analysis using a Python-based script. In
particular, for the continuous-time response shown in Figure~\ref{fig:exp_step_c},
the settling time was approximately \SI{715}{\milli\second} with an overshoot of
2.5\%, which, although still within the specified limits, differs significantly
from the values reported using the TECDigital script. A similar situation was
observed in Figure~\ref{fig:exp_step_d}, where the settling time was approximately
\SI{810}{\milli\second} and the overshoot reached 2.6\%. 

In both cases, the steady-state error remained within the required bounds,
indicating that the integral action was still effective. However, these
differences suggest that the TECDigital script may produce inaccurate estimates
of settling time and overshoot, and therefore may not fully reflect the actual
dynamic response of the controller. This highlights the importance of validating
automated performance metrics with direct analysis of the system response.

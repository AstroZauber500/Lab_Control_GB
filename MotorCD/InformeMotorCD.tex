\section{Introduction}

The control of angular velocity in motor of direct current, is a classic problem of automatic control with a big application in industrials systems and electric conditioning. A typical solution for this type of plant its the proportional-integrative regulator (PI), whose integrative action guarantee a zero steady-state error for inputs like step-type and the capacity of reject disturbances.\cite{ogata}

In this experiment, it is designed a PI regulator for the CD motor system \textit{hps5130}, modelling like a first order system, whose being identify via experimental. The design is performed for both continuous and discrete time with a sampling period of $T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$, meeting the specifications of a settling time of less than 2\% for 300 ms and overshoot of less than 3\%. The results of both designs are verified by simulation in \textit{Simulink}, including the response to disturbances apply to the plant's input and output.\cite{guia}


\section{Theoretical Background}

\subsection{Motor CD as the control plant}

The motor's DC current can be modeled as a first-order linear system in the frequency domain, whose transfer function is related to the armature voltage applied to the output angular speed:
\begin{equation}
    G(s) = \frac{K}{s + p},
    \label{eq:modelo_motor}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $K$ is the static gain and $p$ is the dominant pole, both obtained through experimental identification. \cite{nise}


\subsection{PI regulator}

The PI regulator can be expressed in continuous time as:

\begin{equation}
    PI(s) = K_P + \frac{K_I}{s} = \frac{K_c(s - z_0)}{s},
    \label{eq:pi_continuo}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $K_P$ determines the system's response speed and $K_I$ eliminates the steady-state error for step inputs. In its transfer function form, the PI introduces a pole at the origin $s = 0$ and a zero at $s = z_0$, whose relative location with respect to the plant pole directly influences the closed-loop system dynamics.\cite{nise} 

\subsection{Root-locus method}

The root locus is a graphic technique that allows visualization of the displacement of the poles of the closed-loop in the complex plane $s$, in function of the controller gain. For a system with unity feedback, the poles of the closed-loop satisfy:

\begin{equation}
    1 + K_c \cdot G_{OL}(s) = 0,
    \label{eq:lazo_cerrado}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $G_{OL}(s)$ is the open loop transfer function. The design of the regulator consist in locate the poles of closed-loop in the region's of the plane $s$ that satisfy simultaneously the specifications of settling time and overshoot.\cite{nise} For a second-order system, these specifications impose restrictions on the real part and the angle of the dominant pole, expressed as:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \frac{4}{t_s},
    \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \zeta = \frac{-\ln(M_p/100)}{\sqrt{\pi^2 + \ln^2(M_p/100)}},
    \label{eq:zeta}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\sigma$ is the minimum real part required for the dominant pole, $t_s$ is the settling time to the 2\% and $\zeta$ is the damping coefficient corresponding to the maximum overshoot $M_p$ 

\subsection{Controller discretization}

To implement a PI regulator in a system, the controller in the continuous time has to be convert into discrete time. Using the backward Euler approximation, the complex variable $s$ is replaced by:  

\begin{equation}
    s \approx \frac{z - 1}{T_s \cdot z},
    \label{eq:euler_backward}
\end{equation}

\noindent which transforms the continuous PI into its discrete equivalent:

\begin{equation}
    PI(z) = \frac{b_1 z + b_0}{z + a_0},
    \label{eq:pi_discreto}
\end{equation}

\noindent where the coefficients $b_1$, $b_0$ y $a_0$ depends of $K_P$, $K_I$ and the sampling period $T_s$. The selection of $T_s$ must guarantee between 30 and 75 samples during the stabilization time for an adequate representation of the system dynamics.\cite{ogata}\cite{nise}

\subsection{Performance Criteria}

The design specifications are expressed in terms of three main metrics. 
The 2\% settling time ($t_s$) is defined as the time required for the 
response to remain within a $\pm 2\%$ band around the final value. The 
maximum percent overshoot ($M_p$) represents the largest percentage 
deviation of the response with respect to that final value. Finally, 
the steady-state error is the difference between the reference value 
and the final output value under a step input, which is zero when the 
controller contains an integrator in its structure \cite{ogata}.

\section{Methodology}

This section outlines the design and experimental validation process of 
three PI controllers for the angular velocity regulation of the 
\textit{hps5130} DC motor, in both continuous and discrete time.

\subsection{Plant Model Selection}

Multiple CSV datasets were collected from the \textit{hps5130} motor during 
the identification experiment. A statistical analysis based on the mean and 
standard deviation of the recorded signals was performed to determine the 
most representative dataset, which was subsequently used to obtain the 
empirical first-order plant model via the \textit{System Identification} 
toolbox in MATLAB, following the structure defined in \eqref{eq:modelo_motor}.

\subsection{PI Controller Design}

Three PI controllers were designed in both continuous and discrete time 
using the \textit{sisotool} root locus editor in MATLAB. Each design 
differed in the location of the real zero relative to the dominant plant 
pole, following the three placement criteria described in 
\eqref{eq:pi_continuo}. All designs were constrained to meet a 2\% settling 
time $t_s < \SI{300}{\milli\second}$ and a maximum percent overshoot 
$M_p < \SI{3}{\percent}$. The continuous-time controllers were verified 
in \textit{Simulink} under step input and disturbance rejection conditions, 
while the discrete-time equivalents were obtained at $T_s = \SI{5}{\milli\second}$ 
using the \texttt{c2d} function in MATLAB, but also, were verified in \textit{Simulink} under step input and disturbance rejection conditions.
